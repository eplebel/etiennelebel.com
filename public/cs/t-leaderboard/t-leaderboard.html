Transparency Requirements
*MOST* lenient requirements possible under current ethical codes of conduct for scientists (e.g., NWO).
  • Open Access: Working link to HTML/PDF full-text of article (Gold/Diamond or Green OA, i.e., version of record, copy-edited, no pre-prints) at journal or (co-)author's website, without having to login (so Academia.edu or ResearchGate does *NOT* meet requirement).
  • Open Data: Minimal dataset (for all studies) publicly posted online *OR* valid exemption (e.g., accessible via login/registration for large public datasets; ethical/confidentiality reasons). Personal websites allowed, proprietary files allowed (e.g., SPSS .sav files, STATA files, etc.), no databook required. Working link to dataset required within article full-text, on Curate Scholar author page, or in [co-]author website publication list (completeness/integrity of actual dataset is not verified).
  • Basic Reporting: The following 2 items are disclosed: (1) Any and all (perceived) competing interests/COIs *AND* (2) all funding sources (as already required in more comprehensive reporting guidelines, e.g., CONSORT, JARS). Information must be disclosed in article, on Curate Scholar author page, or in [co-]author website publication list.
  • Curate Science transparency standard: Transparency standard is MET when requirements for *ALL 3* transparency categories are MET (i.e., OA, open data, & basic reporting). Note: This minimum transparency standard is a scaled-back, more lenient version of Curate Science's "starter" transparency standard.
Audit Protocol
For each article, two (2) independent coders (chief curator Etienne P. LeBel and volunteer curator Dominik Lenda) audited the transparency status of each of the 3 transparency categories (OA, open data, and basic reporting) for ALL peer-reviewed, empirical articles published by each author between 2015 to 2021 (or older for early adopters). This was done by searching within the full-texts of articles, which were found via Google Scholar, on author's personal websites, or author's Curate Scholar author page. When OA full-text was NOT available, we used either a Sci-Hub PDF or preprint to audit the open data and basic reporting categories (indicated via hyperlinked Sci-Hub icon to the right of article titles, e.g., ). When in doubt, we erred on the side of being overly generous/charitable, given that (1) we're in a transition period, and (2) being generous in meeting a transparency standard is still better than NOT meeting *any* transparency standard AT ALL.
Rationale/Motivations
Science works because it requires transparency, which allows independent scrutiny of claims/evidence. Indeed, this is why minimum transparency is a core ethical principle scientists are expected to uphold (e.g., NWO code of conduct). Minimum transparency ensures scientists are accountable (1) to the entities who fund their research and (2) to the people whose lives are being impacted by their published findings. It is in this spirit, that we present our transparency leaderboard, based on the first ever (to our knowledge) researcher transparency audit.
FAQ
How were authors chosen for the transparency audit?
Authors were chosen either because they are (1) transparency advocates/leaders/early adopters and/or (2) are high-impact/high-visibility researchers. This is a "proof of concept" exercise as part of our targeted transparency audits. Soon, we will begin random audits of all researchers (population frame = Google Scholar and all major open access journals).
What authority does Curate Science have to do transparency audits?
Curate Science does NOT technically have any official authority. However, our transparency audits, and transparency standards, are based on foundational first principles, and current ethical principles that all researchers are expected to abide by (e.g., NWO code of conduct). We also practice what we preach, i.e., accountability through transparency, committed to conducting our transparency audits transparently (and correcting errors/inaccuracies swiftly).
How were the minimum requirements for the transparency standards chosen?
Minimum requirements were chosen as to be as generous/lenient as possible (i.e., we chose the fewest possible requirements) while still conforming to current ethical codes of conduct scientists are expected to abide by (e.g., NWO).
But isn't there still some arbitrariness in these minimum standards?
To some extent yes, but it is still better for the research community to have its researcher meet a minimum transparency standard with small amounts of arbitrariness, than NOT meeting any transparency standard at all (the current situation).
What are the benefits of transparency audits?
Tracking the transparency track record of researchers via transparency audits offers a multitude of benefits for ALL research stakeholders (i.e., researchers, journals, universities, funding agencies, and citizens). For example, researchers can signal their transparency track record on their website/CV, universities can use job candidate's transparency track record in hiring/promotion decision, and funding agencies can use this in grant application/renewal decisions (and several other applications).
How can I add missing transparency information to my published articles?
You can do so by adding the information to your publication list via a Curate Scholar author page, or on your own website (more details on this coming soon).
