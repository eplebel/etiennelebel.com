Transparency Requirements
*MOST* lenient requirements possible under current ethical codes of conduct for scientists (e.g., NWO, ALLEA).
  1. Open Access (OA): Working link to article HTML/PDF full-text (version-of-record) at journal or (co-)author's website. Details:
    • Gold/Diamond or Green+ OA: "Green+ OA" means "Green OA" + copy-edited, required to (1) read/comprehend/scrutinize an article more easily and (2) to legitimize the authenticity of a publication (mitigating predatory journal problem). Also, for publicly-funded research - most research - the public is entitled to the final copy-edited product that they paid for.
    • Full-text access without having to login (hence, no Academia.edu/ResearchGate links)
    • PubMed Central/Europe PMC OA articles allowed (exemption given authoritative & own copyediting)
    • No pre-prints
  2. Open Data: Minimal dataset (for all studies) publicly posted online. Working link to dataset included within article full-text, on Curate Scholar author page, or in [co-]author website publication list (completeness of dataset(s) NOT verified). Allowed:
    • Personal websites allowed
    • Proprietary files allowed (e.g., SPSS .sav files, STATA .do files, etc.)
    • Databook/codebook NOT required
    Valid exemptions:
    • Accessible via registration for large public datasets (URL to dataset registration must be provided)
    • Ethical/confidentiality reasons (exemption evidence needed, e.g., consent form)
  3. Basic Disclosures: Following 2 disclosures made in article, on Curate Scholar author page, or in [co-]author website publication list (as done in reporting guidelines, e.g., CONSORT, JARS):
    1. Any, and all, (potentially) competing interests (financial or non-financial) that occurred within 5 years of conducting the research/preparing an article. Disclosures must be *specific* and *comprehensive* (see list of example financial/non-financial COIs that must be declared; see example COI disclosure statements that MEET vs. DO NOT MEET the *specificity* and *comprehensiveness* requirements.).✝
    2. Names of any, and all, funding sources/research grants received
      3. ✝"Grandfather" provisions: Any pre-existing vague and/or non-comprehensive COI disclosures are "grandfathered" to MEET the standard. However, once an author is audited, specific and comprehensive COI disclosures must be made in future publications.

Audit Protocol
The following steps are taken to audit the transparency of authors' articles:
  1. Identify articles: First, eligible articles are identified by searching Google Scholar and the author's personal website, for any, and all, peer-reviewed empirical articles published in the last 5 years (for 1st audit, this corresponded to articles published between Jan 1, 2015 and Jan 27, 2021; or prior to 2015 for early adopters; minimum N=10 articles).
  2. Check transparency: The transparency status (OA, open data, & COI disclosures) of each article is checked by searching for the relevant transparency information within the article full-text (a Sci-Hub PDF or a preprint is used if OA full-text NOT available). This is done by 1 or 2 auditors (for the 1st audit, Etienne P. LeBel extracted transparency information for all articles; volunteer Dominik Lenda, independently coded a subset of each author's articles (3-5 articles/author; N=34 total); all discrepancies (13) were easily resolved, see spreedsheet).
  3. Author verification: Audited researchers are then given an opportunity to verify/revise/add to their transparency information BEFORE audit results are publicly posted (via email, 2-week response window, 2 reminders maximum).
  4. Post audit results: If audited researchers provide additional transparency information or corrections, the information is updated on the website, and authors are given a final opportunity to check the correctness of the updates before public release of audit results.
Rationale/Motivations
Science works because it requires transparency, which allows independent scrutiny of claims and evidence. In fact, one can say that transparency is the price of admission to enter the arena of science. Indeed, this is why minimum transparency is a core ethical principle scientists are expected to uphold in their research (e.g., NWO code of conduct). It is in this spirit that we are conducting these (global first) researcher transparency audits.
FAQ
1. How were authors chosen for the first round of transparency audits?
Authors were chosen either because they are (1) transparency advocates/leaders/early adopters and/or (2) are high-impact/high-visibility researchers previously involved in open-science/replication-related discussions. This is a "proof of concept" exercise as part of our targeted transparency audits, which targets high-impact and publicly-funded researchers. Soon, we will begin random audits based on randomly selected researchers from a broader population frame (i.e., PubMed, Europe PMC, Microsoft Academic Knowledge API, Google Scholar, and major open access journals).
2. What authority does Curate Science have to do transparency audits?
Curate Science does NOT technically have any official authority. However, our transparency audits, and transparency standards, are based on foundational first principles, and current ethical principles that all researchers are expected to abide by in their research (e.g., NWO code of conduct; NIH code of conduct). We also practice what we preach by conducting our transparency audits transparently (in fact, the curated article-level transparency information displayed in the leaderboard table makes it easy to verify the correctness of the audit results). Additionally, audit results go through various quality control processes PRIOR to public dissemination (e.g., author-verified checks), and we're committed to swiftly correcting any flagged errors/inaccuracies.
3. Who audits the auditors?
Given that we strive to conduct our transparency audits transparently, this means anyone can independently verify the correctness of our audits. In fact, we've intentionally "surfaced" article-level transparency information within the leaderboard table itself to make it easy for independent parties to verify the correctness of audited article's transparency information. Moving forward, we will further increase the transparency of our audit process, e.g., open database of all auditor-auditee interactions and other audit metadata (upon securing additional funding).
4. How were the minimum requirements for the transparency standards chosen?
Minimum requirements were chosen to be as generous/lenient as possible (i.e., we chose the fewest possible requirements) while still conforming to current ethical codes of conduct scientists are expected to abide by. For example, public posting of data when ethically possible (NWO/Netherlands, Section "2. Principles", Subsection "3. Transparency", sentence 2: "If parts of the research or data are *NOT* to be made public, the researcher must provide a good account of why this is not possible."; NIH/United States, Section "Publications", paragraph 3, sentence 2: The NIH position on reproducibility states that *ALL* essential data should be included in the published paper or be deposited in appropriate public databases or made available online.) and declaring any and all competing interests that could impair objectivity/impartiality (NWO/Netherlands, Section "3.4 Reporting Results", point 44: Be open and complete about the role of external stakeholders, commissioning parties, funding bodies, possible conflicts of interest and relevant ancillary activities.; ALLEA/European Union, Section "2.7 Publication & Dissemination", paragraph 5: All authors disclose any conflicts of interest and financial or other types of support for the research or for the publication of its results.).
5. But isn't there still some arbitrariness in these minimum standards?
To some extent yes, but it is still better for the research community to have its researchers meet a minimum transparency standard with small amounts of arbitrariness, than NOT meeting any transparency standard at all (the current situation).

8. Is it really feasible to do transparency audits at a larger/broader scale?
Though challenging, it *IS* currently feasible to conduct transparency audits at scale given various new technologies and tools at our disposal. Transparency standards exist that can flexibly be used across article types and fields. Web crawling (e.g., Scrapy) and text-mining technologies can be used to automate as much of the transparency metadata extraction/harvesting as possible from OA articles/PDFs. This includes the following (open-source) tools:
  • Open access: Swiss National Science Foundation's Open Access check tool to determine open access status
  • Open data: QUEST's (Germany) Open Data Detection in Publications (ODDPub) text-mining tool to extract open data status from data availability statements
  • COI/funding disclosures: Serghiou et al.'s (2021) "rtransparent" R package that automatically extracts COI/funding disclosure statements from PDF/XML articles
9. Is research NOT meeting the minimum transparency standard *less* trustworthy?
Generally, yes, but not necessarily so. Research that meets the minimum transparency standard, all else being equal, *is* more likely to be trustworthy compared to research NOT meeting a transparency standard. This is the case because (1) transparent research is easier to independently scrutinize, and (2) because reporting research transparently makes researchers think twice about publishing shaky findings given the realization that errors will be more easily identified. That said, transparent research is NOT automatically MORE trustworthy because it is still possible that transparent research contains errors that have not yet been identified (though mitigating against this is that regularly publishing transparent research containing major errors will eventually negatively influence a researcher's reputation).
10. What's this business with "grandfathering" pre-existing COI disclosures?
To smoothen the transition toward greater transparency in disclosing financial/non-financial COIs, we offer "grandfathered provisions" to audited authors' pre-existing COI disclosures. This means that a pre-existing VAGUE and/or NON-COMPREHENSIVE COI disclosure is said to "meet" our standard. Once an author has been audited, however, their COI disclosures in FUTURE publications will need to meet the NEW (more transparent) standard, which requires SPECIFIC and COMPREHENSIVE financial COI disclosures. *SPECIFIC* means disclosing specific details of each COI separately (e.g., speaking fees, consultant fees, stock ownership, etc; see list of example financial/non-financial COIs) rather than disclosing a general category of COIs. *COMPREHENSIVE* means disclosing any, and all, financial and non-financial COIs, even if a researcher believes a financial COI is NOT relevant to the reported research.
Grandfather provisions are offered because (1) many researchers have NOT received formal/proper ethics training, and (2) because humans are vulnerable to various cognitive and social psychological biases which leads them to justify VAGUE and NON-COMPREHENSIVE disclosures as sufficient.
Example #1:
VAGUE COI disclosure (meets standard under GRANDFATHER clause):
John Smith is a consultant for Beneficial Nonprofit, a nonprofit organization with a mission to develop and deliver methods for making the world a better place.
SPECIFIC COI disclosure (meets NEW standard):
John Smith is a paid consultant who regularly receives consulting fees for giving speeches and workshops for Beneficial Nonprofit, a nonprofit organization with a mission to develop and deliver methods for making the world a better place.
Example #2:
VAGUE and NON-COMPREHENSIVE COI disclosure (meets standard under GRANDFATHER clause):
John Smith is an employee for Ethical Nonprofit, a nonprofit organization with a mission to improve ethics in the workplace.
SPECIFIC and COMPREHENSIVE COI disclosure (meets NEW standard):
John Smith is a paid employee (executive director) for Ethical Nonprofit, a nonprofit organization with a mission to improve ethics in the workplace. John Smith is also a paid consultant who regularly receives consulting fees for giving speeches and workshops for Beneficial Nonprofit, a nonprofit organization with a mission to develop and deliver methods for making the world a better place.
11. But what happens if one of my co-authors does *NOT* want to publicy post data to meet the transparency standard?
As a co-author you "benefit" from a publication, but this means you also must share responsibility in terms of transparency and accountability. Of course, you cannot "force" a co-author/lead author to publicly post data, but you can try to convince them. In fact, this is an additional benefit of transparency audits: An audited author will have a heightened motivation to convince their co-authors to be more transparent by presenting various arguments to them (e.g., citing selfish reasons like the open data citation advantage, basic scientific hygiene, ethical duty, etc.). This also means researchers might become more judicious in co-authoring a paper with other researchers who may not share the same ethical values regarding data sharing.
