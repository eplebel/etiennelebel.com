Transparency Requirements
  • Open Access = Working link to HTML/PDF full-text of article (Gold/Diamond or Green OA, i.e., version of record, copy-edited, no pre-prints) at journal or author's (or any co-author's) website, without having to login (so Academia.edu or ResearchGate does *NOT* meet requirement).
  • Open Data = Minimum dataset (for all studies) publicly posted online (personal websites allowed), no databook required, proprietary files allowed (e.g., SPSS .sav files, STATA files, etc.), and valid exemptions allowed (e.g., protected access login, ethical/confidential reasons). Working link to dataset required within article full-text or on Curate Scholar author page (completeness/integrity of actual dataset not verified).
  • Basic Reporting = The following 2 diclosures must be made in article or on Curate Scholar author page: (1) any and all (perceived) competing interests/COIs *AND* (2) all funding sources.
  • Curate Science Transparency standard = Meeting requirements for *ALL 3* basic transparency categories (i.e., OA, open data, & basic reporting; which is a scaled back version of Curate Science's official "starter" transparency standard).
Audit Protocol
Two (2) independent curators/coders (chief curator Etienne P. LeBel and volunteer curator Dominik Lenda) audited the transparency status of each article with respect to the 3 transparency categories (OA, open data, and basic reporting). This was done by scanning/searching within article full-texts found via Google Scholar, on author's personal websites, or author's Curate Scholar author page. When OA full-text NOT available, we used either a Sci-Hub PDF or preprint to audit the open data and basic reporting categories (indicated via hyperlinked Sci-Hub icon to the right of article titles, e.g., ). When in doubt, we erred on the side of being overly generous/charitable (given that 1. we're in a transition/transformation period, and 2. being generous in meeting a low transparency standard is still better than NOT meeting *any* standard).
Rationale/Motivations
Science works because transparency allows for the independent checking of the correctness of claims/credibility of evidence. Indeed, this is why minimum transparency is a core ethical principle scientists are expected to uphold (e.g., NWO code of conduct). Minimum transparency ensures scientists are accountable (1) to the entities who fund their research and (2) to the people whose lives are being impacted by scientific findings. It is in this spirit, that we present our transparency leaderboard, based on the first ever (to our knowledge) researcher transparency audit.
